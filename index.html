Honey Bee Colonies

United States: January-March, 2019

Written By: Coe MacFarlane
November 1, 2020

Public awareness around Colony Collapse Disorder has increased drastically over the past several years along with the rate at which these colonies are dying. This serious disorder is caused by a slew of complex environmental factors but societal changes and public programs that support bee populations across America are helping to combat this phenomenon. On January 1, 2019 Honey Bee colonies for operations numbering 5 or more were up 1% from the previous year but it is unclear if this is the start of a true recovery or a happy but temporary uptick. As of July 2019, quarterly data collection by y the National Agricultural Statistics Service (NASS) has been suspended due to a lack of budget. I have elected to analyze some of the data made public in their final quarter of operation and make digestable the raw numbers.

Percentage of Bee Colonies Lost Compared to Number of Colonies in January

The map above illustrates the American States and their corresponding rates of Honey Bee Colonies lost from January to March calculated as a percentage out of the initial number of colonies alive on January first. Darker colors indicate a higher rate of colonies lost to colony collapse disorder. Those which are pale white and do not have numbers listed in the window which appears when you hover over, are states which the government was unable to collect data for.

Drastic Increase in Colony Numbers

From January 1st to some point in the quarter these states saw an increase in their number of hives. This chart displays the ten states which have the greatest difference between their number of colonies at the beginning of the quarter and their maximum number of hives at any given point in the quarter. The Lost to Added Colony Ratios

This chart contextualizes the percentage of colonies lost in a state out of their initial number of colonies to the similarly calculated percentage of colonies added. These ten states had the greatest difference between these percentages. Since all states did lose more colonies than could be added, I have here displayed the most drastic instances of these phenomenon. For the United States in its entirety, 407700 colonies were lost to colony collapse disorder and 247710 were added.
